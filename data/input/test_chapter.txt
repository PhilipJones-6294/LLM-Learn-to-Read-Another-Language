CHAPTER ONE
The Lesson Begins

It was a quiet morning at Thornfield Academy. The old stone corridors were 
empty at this hour, and the only sound was the distant echo of footsteps on 
the flagstone floor. Professor Marlowe stood at the window of her classroom, 
watching the fog settle over the grounds below.

"Good morning," said Clara, dropping her bag by the nearest desk.

Professor Marlowe turned from the window. She had been teaching here for 
thirty years, and she still found the morning fog beautiful in a way she could 
not quite explain.

"Good morning," said Clara, sitting down and pulling out her notebook.

The other students filed in one by one. James was first, as always, taking his 
place in the front row with the careful precision of someone who had decided 
long ago that the front row was where serious people sat. Then came Eli, then 
Priya, then the three students whose names Professor Marlowe still sometimes 
confused, though she would never admit it.

"Good morning," said Clara, this time to no one in particular.

Professor Marlowe cleared her throat importantly. She crossed the room and 
stood at the lectern, arranging her notes with the slow deliberateness of 
someone who understood that the arrangement of notes was itself a form of 
authority.

"Today," she said, "we begin something different."

No one spoke. The classroom had that particular morning stillness that Clara 
had come to think of as the silence before things changed.

"Good morning," said James, arriving late and slightly out of breath.

Professor Marlowe looked at him over the top of her glasses. She did not 
say anything. She did not need to. James found his seat.

The lesson began. Professor Marlowe spoke for twenty minutes without pause, 
covering the history of the subject in broad strokes — the origins, the early 
failures, the long decades during which no one had believed it was possible, 
and then the sudden unexpected moment when everything had shifted. Clara 
took notes. James took notes more slowly, pressing too hard with his pen. 
Eli did not take notes at all but stared at the ceiling with an expression 
that Professor Marlowe had learned to recognize as intense concentration 
rather than boredom, though it had taken her three years to learn this.

"Good morning," said Clara, reading back what she had written by mistake.

Everyone laughed except Professor Marlowe, who smiled only slightly, which 
was her version of laughing.

She crossed the room again, this time to the far wall where the old map hung. 
It was a large map, older than the building itself, showing routes and territories 
that no longer existed in quite the same form. She pressed her hand flat against 
the paper and traced one of the routes with her finger.

"This is where we are," she said quietly.

Clara leaned forward.
James leaned forward.
Priya leaned forward.

"Good morning," said Clara softly, as if testing whether the words still meant 
what they had meant at the beginning of the hour.

Professor Marlowe did not look up from the map.

"We begin here," she said. "We always begin here."

The fog outside had lifted slightly. Through the tall windows Clara could see 
the grounds more clearly now — the old oak at the edge of the courtyard, the 
bench where students sat in summer, the iron gate that was always locked but 
which everyone assumed opened onto something worth seeing.

"Good morning," said James quietly, to himself, as though he were practicing.

It was not morning any more, strictly speaking. But no one corrected him.

Professor Marlowe turned back to face the class. She looked at them — really 
looked at them — in the way she sometimes did when she was about to say 
something she considered important.

"You will find," she said, "that it is easier than you expect."

Clara wrote this down. James wrote it down more slowly. Eli continued to 
stare at the ceiling, but his pen moved.

"Good morning," said Clara, quietly, reading it back again.

Professor Marlowe set down her notes. She walked to the door. She paused 
with her hand on the frame, as she always did at the end of a lesson, and 
she looked back at the class one final time.

"Good morning," she said. And then she left.

The students sat in silence for a moment.

Then James said it too.
Then Priya.

"Good morning," said Clara, last of all, and meant it.

The fog had lifted entirely by the time they gathered their things. Through 
the windows the grounds were bright and ordinary and exactly what they had 
always been. The iron gate was still locked. The bench was still empty. 
The old oak stood where it had always stood, which was the most reliable 
thing about it.

They filed out one by one, the same way they had come in.

The classroom was quiet again.
Professor Marlowe's notes remained on the lectern.
The map was still on the wall.

And on the board, in her careful handwriting, were three words she had written 
before any of them arrived, and which none of them had thought to read:

We begin here.
